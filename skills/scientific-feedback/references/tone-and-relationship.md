# Tone And Relationship

Use this when feedback must fit a role relationship: peer, trainee, mentor, PI, collaborator, supervisor, reviewer, editor, committee member, or applicant coach.

## Use this when...

- The user asks for comments to send to someone else.
- There is a power difference, evaluation role, conflict, sensitivity, or mentoring context.
- The same technical feedback could be framed as review, coaching, collaboration, or decision support.

## The job of this topic

Make feedback candid and useful without misrepresenting the relationship, escalating unnecessarily, or removing the user's agency.

## Default workflow

1. Identify relationship and stakes.
2. Decide whether feedback should be direct critique, coaching, collaborative revision, or formal assessment.
3. Keep the substance specific while adapting the tone.
4. Separate observations from judgments and suggested actions.
5. For power-sensitive contexts, include discussion questions, support, expectations, or escalation boundaries as appropriate.

## Decision rules

- Reviewer voice: concise, evidence-linked, prioritized, and bounded to the artifact.
- Mentor voice: developmental, direct, and action-oriented; explain why the change matters.
- Collaborator voice: shared-goal framing plus concrete tradeoffs.
- Supervisor or PI voice: observable expectations, support, follow-up, and role clarity.
- Committee voice: readiness, criteria, risks, and decision implications.
- Self-revision voice: direct triage and next action without performative politeness.

## Variants and edge cases

- If feedback includes personnel, conflict, distress, harassment, retaliation, legal, or institutional policy issues, route through the mentoring-management-and-lab-culture lens and avoid overclaiming.
- If feedback will be pasted into a peer review, keep confidential or editor-only content out of author-facing text.
- If the user wants "harsh" feedback, make it frank and prioritized rather than insulting.
- If the artifact is from a trainee, preserve learning goals and avoid rewriting everything in the assistant's voice.

## Anti-patterns

- Softening feedback until the needed action disappears.
- Making feedback personal instead of artifact-specific.
- Giving reviewer-style verdicts in a mentoring relationship.
- Hiding power dynamics behind "just be honest."
- Writing comments that expose confidential context or private provenance.

## Diagnostic questions

- Who will read this feedback?
- What authority or obligation does the user have in this relationship?
- Is the goal revision, evaluation, coaching, or documentation?
- Which comments should be private discussion rather than written markup?
- What support or follow-up should accompany hard feedback?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

```text
Relationship: <role>.
Tone target: <reviewer/mentor/collaborator/supervisor/committee>.
Core message: <specific artifact issue>.
Suggested wording: <sendable comment>.
Follow-up: <question, support, or next checkpoint>.
```

## Examples

- Reviewer: "The central claim is plausible, but the current evidence does not separate mechanism A from mechanism B. Please narrow the claim or add the comparison."
- Mentor: "The strongest result is buried. Let's move it earlier and use the weaker results as support, not as the opening frame."
- Collaborator: "I think the poster should trade one methods block for a larger result panel because the audience needs to read the result at walking distance."

## When not to apply this

Do not use tone adaptation to avoid substantive critique. Use it to make the critique usable for the relationship.
