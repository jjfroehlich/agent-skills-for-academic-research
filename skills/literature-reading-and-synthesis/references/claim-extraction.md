# Claim Extraction

Use this when the user needs to identify claims, evidence, methods, limitations, figure logic, or whether a result is strong enough to reuse.

## Use this when...

- The user asks what a paper claims or how strong the evidence is.
- A figure, table, experiment, or results section needs unpacking.
- The user is preparing journal-club, presentation, or literature-review notes.
- The user needs a table that separates claims from support and interpretation.

## The job of this topic

Turn reading into structured evidence review. Do not produce a generic summary. Separate what the authors wanted to know, what they did, why that method was appropriate, what the data show, how the authors interpret it, and what should happen next.

## Default workflow

1. Name the evidence unit: whole paper, figure, table, experiment, or section.
2. Ask six questions:
   - Why was this done?
   - What was done?
   - Why was it done that way?
   - What was found?
   - What do the authors think it means?
   - What should happen next?
3. For each major claim, record the supporting evidence object.
4. Separate results from interpretation.
5. Check alternative explanations, bias, limitations, confounders, and generalizability.
6. Write a short reuse decision: use, use with caveat, background only, or do not rely on it yet.

## Decision rules

- If a paper is central to a research decision, inspect methods, key supplemental material, and key citations before trusting the claim.
- If a figure is hard to understand, stop summarizing and decode the figure before writing the take-home.
- If the article is a review, extract field map and major positions rather than forcing a primary-research claim table.
- If the article is a method or resource paper, extract procedure, validation, scope, and reuse constraints.
- If critique focuses on wording or citation irritation, recenter on evidence, design, bias, limitations, and generalizability.

## Variants and edge cases

- **Methods paper:** Procedure, assumptions, validation, input requirements, failure modes, and comparison baseline matter more than narrative arc.
- **Review article:** Extract the map of positions, debates, source clusters, and gaps.
- **Resource paper:** Extract data/tool scope, access conditions, quality controls, and appropriate reuse cases.
- **Controversial topic:** Record the conflict, competing interpretations, and whether the authors acknowledge uncertainty.
- **Low-priority alert:** Capture claim, evidence type, and relevance, then defer deeper appraisal.

## Anti-patterns

- Copying the abstract as the note.
- Recording the authors' interpretation without the evidence.
- Treating a publication as settled truth.
- Treating minor errors as fatal while ignoring design quality.
- Mixing claim, method, result, limitation, and speculation in one field.

## Diagnostic questions

- Can the note answer all six extraction questions?
- Is each major claim tied to a concrete evidence object?
- Are result and interpretation separate?
- Are limitations, bias, confounders, and generalizability recorded?
- Does the note say what the user should do with the claim?
- Is criticism substantive rather than mostly stylistic?

## Examples

- For a central figure, decode the axes, comparisons, groups, statistics, legend, and method link before writing "this supports X."
- For a journal-club critique, list alternative explanations and confounders before deciding whether the claim is weak.
- For a methods paper, focus on what was validated and where the method may fail.

## When not to apply this

- Do not run full claim extraction when the user only needs a quick relevance screen.
- Do not use this as a peer-review template; use it to understand and synthesize evidence.
