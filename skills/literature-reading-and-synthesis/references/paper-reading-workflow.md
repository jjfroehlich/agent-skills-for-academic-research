# Paper Reading Workflow

Use this when the user needs an active strategy for reading one or more scientific papers.

## Use this when...

- The user needs to present or discuss a paper.
- The user is entering a new field.
- The user needs to decide how deeply to read a paper.
- The user has many papers and needs triage plus selected deep reading.
- The user wants a repeatable reading pass rather than a one-off summary.

## The job of this topic

Match reading depth to the user's goal and article type. A paper for tomorrow's presentation, a method lookup, a new-field survey, and a low-priority alert should not be read the same way.

## Default workflow

1. Name the goal: orient, present, decide, compare, track, or synthesize.
2. Pick depth:
   - **Triage:** title, abstract, figures, relevance, save/ignore decision.
   - **Targeted read:** focus on sections needed for the goal.
   - **Evidence read:** unpack claims, figures, methods, and limitations.
   - **Deep read:** add citation follow-up, supplemental checks, and discussion prep.
3. Classify article type: primary research, review, methods paper, resource paper, or commentary.
4. Use section intent:
   - Introduction: problem, motivation, field gap.
   - Methods: design, reproducibility, assumptions.
   - Results/figures: evidence.
   - Discussion: interpretation, limitations, future work.
   - References/supplement: follow-up trail.
5. Extract claims and evidence with the six-question scaffold.
6. End with a synthesis action: add to matrix, follow citation, update field map, or mark as background.

## Decision rules

- If the paper is for tomorrow's presentation, use a high-depth read: goal, article type, six questions, major figures, limitations, and discussion prompts.
- If the paper is central to a research decision, add the extra-mile pass: terms, methods, supplement, and key citations.
- If the paper is a review, use it to map the field rather than forcing result-by-result extraction.
- If the paper is a method or resource, prioritize procedure, validation, scope, and reuse constraints.
- If the paper is only an alert, record relevance and next action rather than deep-reading immediately.

## Variants and edge cases

- **New-field survey:** Prioritize motivation, article type, vocabulary, references, and major positions.
- **Presentation prep:** Prepare the narrative, key figures, strongest claim, limitations, and two discussion questions.
- **Method lookup:** Find assumptions, inputs, validation, comparison baseline, and failure modes.
- **Project decision:** Track what the paper changes about the user's project, not just what it says.
- **Dense article:** Use multiple passes: overview, evidence/method detail, then synthesis notes.

## Anti-patterns

- Reading without first naming the goal.
- Reading every article at the same depth.
- Copying abstract sentences as notes.
- Skipping figures because the prose seems clear.
- Stopping at comprehension when the user needs a next research action.

## Diagnostic questions

- What does the user need to do after reading?
- Which article type is this?
- Which sections are required for the goal?
- Can the user explain the paper at both high and evidence-detail levels?
- What needs a deeper follow-up pass?
- What changes in the user's field map, project, or synthesis?

### Three-pass plan

1. **Pass 1: orientation.** Goal, article type, abstract, figures, conclusion, rough relevance.
2. **Pass 2: understanding.** Six questions, section intent, key methods, major figures, limitations.
3. **Pass 3: synthesis.** Citation trail, build-on-it note, matrix row, discussion prompts.

## Examples

- For a journal-club paper due tomorrow, skip broad source hunting and build an evidence-read plan around the key figures.
- For a new-field stack of papers, start with review and orientation goals before deep-reading every experiment.
- For a method paper, the output should be procedure and validation notes, not only a narrative summary.

## When not to apply this

- Do not use a full paper-reading workflow when the user only asks whether a paper is relevant enough to save.
- Do not route citation-formatting requests here.
