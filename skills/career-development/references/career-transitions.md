# Career Transitions

Use this when the user is comparing academia, biotech, industry, editing, policy, business, consulting, entrepreneurship, or another path, or when they are deciding whether to leave a PhD, postdoc, lab, or academic track.

## The job of this topic

Move the user from vague attraction or fear to evidence-based comparison. Career-transition guidance should compare daily work, incentives, constraints, entry evidence, and reversible next experiments.

## Default workflow

1. Clarify the paths under comparison and the user's hard constraints: money, location, visa, family, health, timing, risk tolerance, values, and identity.
2. Define each option by daily work, problems solved, stakeholders, outputs, success metrics, collaboration patterns, and time horizons.
3. Translate the user's scientific training into target-role evidence.
4. Identify missing evidence and design small tests: informational interviews, job-ad analysis, short projects, coursework, internships, shadowing, or targeted networking.
5. Separate acute distress from durable mismatch when the user is considering leaving a path.
6. For offers or new roles, rank must-haves and nice-to-haves, then translate the first month into relationships, commitments, feedback loops, and early evidence of fit.
7. End with a comparison table, experiment plan, translated materials, negotiation plan, first-month plan, or decision memo.

## Decision rules

- If a role sounds attractive only by title, collect daily-work and success-metric evidence before recommending it.
- If the user is considering leaving a PhD or academic path, map reversible and irreversible steps, support, finances, visa status, and fallback options before action.
- If the transition requires missing skills, build a focused bridge project or training plan rather than a broad self-improvement list.
- If the user wants industry, translate research evidence into business, product, team, regulatory, communication, or operational language where relevant.
- If prestige and fit conflict, name the tradeoff and ask which constraint the user is willing to pay for.
- If an offer exists, negotiate from ranked priorities, market evidence, workable terms, and written confirmation rather than from a vague desire for "more."
- If the user is starting a role, plan early integration around relationships, expectations, feedback, and signals of structural fit or mismatch.

## Variants and Edge Cases

- For biotech roles, compare team orientation, milestones, funding runway, product or platform model, and tolerance for changing priorities.
- For leaving-PhD decisions, include mental-health and safety support without pretending to provide clinical or legal advice.
- For informational interviews, ask about daily work, success metrics, frustrations, hiring criteria, and examples of people who thrive or leave.
- For networking through meetings, choose venues by relevance, access to target people, visibility, and cost; follow up with context.
- For biotech roles, avoid generalizing from one company: company stage, financing, product risk, leadership norms, and role family can change the work.
- For non-faculty negotiation, stay within the user's leverage and safety: timing, norms, visa constraints, and local labor conditions matter.

## Anti-Patterns

- Treating academia and industry as single monolithic choices.
- Using salary, prestige, or title as the only comparison.
- Applying to many roles without understanding the evidence each role requires.
- Framing a transition as failure rather than a decision under constraints.
- Asking people for jobs before asking useful, specific questions.
- Treating a first month as passive onboarding instead of an active evidence-gathering and relationship-building period.
- Assuming every offer term is negotiable or safe to raise in every context.

## Diagnostic Questions

- What does the user want their ordinary workweek to contain or avoid?
- Which constraints are nonnegotiable, and which are preferences?
- What evidence does the target role need that the user's academic materials do not yet show?
- Who can describe the role from direct experience?
- What small experiment would reduce uncertainty fastest?
- Which offer terms are must-haves, which are tradeable, and what evidence justifies each ask?
- What should the user learn in the first month that would confirm fit or reveal a structural mismatch?

## Output Patterns / Mini-Templates

Career comparison row:

| Path | Daily work | Success metrics | Entry evidence | Fit signals | Risks | Next experiment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Biotech scientist | Team experiments toward product milestones | Reproducible data, decisions, timelines | Methods plus collaboration stories | Likes team execution | Less autonomy | Interview two recent hires |

Leaving-path decision memo:

1. Current problem: acute crisis, durable mismatch, structural risk, or unclear evidence.
2. Constraints: financial, visa, health, family, timing.
3. Options: stay with changes, pause, transfer, master out, leave, or parallel search.
4. Evidence still needed.
5. Lowest-regret next step.

Offer negotiation plan:

| Ask | Why it matters | Evidence or benchmark | Priority | Possible tradeoff |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Start date | Allows relocation and project handoff | Current contract end date | Must-have | Later review date |

First-month integration plan:

| Area | Action | Evidence of progress | Risk signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Key relationships | Meet manager, peers, and collaborators | Expectations and communication norms are explicit | Conflicting priorities with no owner |

## Examples

- Strong transition evidence: a project that shows the user can solve target-role problems with relevant constraints.
- Weak transition evidence: "I have a PhD, so I can do anything" without role-specific translation.
- Good informational question: "What does a strong first six months look like in this role, and what backgrounds struggle?"
- Strong negotiation framing: "I am asking for this term because it reduces a specific execution or transition risk, and I can trade on lower-priority items."
- Strong first-month plan: clarify success measures, keep commitments small and visible, ask for early feedback, and document uncertainties without overreacting.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook for routine CV formatting or cover-letter drafting unless the main problem is translating across career paths.
