# Advisor Choice

Use this when the user is evaluating a PhD advisor, postdoc mentor, rotation lab, thesis committee member, or research group.

## The job of this topic

Help the user choose a training environment using evidence about mentoring behavior, power, safety, resources, and career fit. The goal is not to identify the most famous lab; it is to identify a place where the user can do good work, develop, and stay safe enough to make progress.

## Default workflow

1. Clarify stage and commitment level: prospective student, rotation, accepted offer, current trainee, postdoc applicant, or trainee in difficulty.
2. Define the user's nonnegotiables: field fit, geography, funding, visa status, family constraints, mental health, methods, independence, and career direction.
3. Compare advisors on observable behavior: meeting cadence, feedback, project choice, authorship, publication support, conflict handling, funding stability, and alumni outcomes.
4. Triangulate evidence from current trainees, former trainees, peers outside the lab, public outputs, and direct advisor answers.
5. Separate yellow flags from red flags and name what evidence would resolve each uncertainty.
6. End with questions to ask, people to contact, a risk matrix, or a decision recommendation with caveats.

## Decision rules

- If the science is exciting but trainees describe fear, retaliation, isolation, credit problems, or ignored boundaries, prioritize safety and fallback options over project appeal.
- If current trainees are uniformly positive, still ask former trainees and adjacent lab members; the visible trainees may be the people for whom the lab already worked.
- If an advisor promises autonomy, ask for examples of how projects, authorship, and independence developed for recent trainees.
- If a lab depends on one grant, one project, or one advisor relationship, check what happens if funding, project direction, or fit changes.
- If the user is already in a harmful relationship, focus on documentation, neutral advice, institutional channels, trusted mentors, and practical exits rather than debating whether the behavior "counts."

## Variants and Edge Cases

- For rotations, optimize for direct observation: how quickly feedback arrives, how people talk to each other, how mistakes are handled, and whether expectations are explicit.
- For international trainees, include contract, visa, residence, funding, and institutional dependency in the risk assessment.
- For postdocs, add independence, project portability, job-market support, and whether the PI will let the trainee take a direction forward.
- For co-advising, ask who controls funding, authorship, meeting structure, conflict resolution, and final decisions.

## Anti-Patterns

- Choosing only by topic match, institutional brand, or publication venue.
- Asking "Is the advisor nice?" instead of asking for specific examples of behavior under pressure.
- Treating gossip and direct trainee evidence as the same kind of signal.
- Ignoring repeated vague answers about funding, authorship, expectations, or conflict.
- Advising confrontation when the user has not assessed retaliation risk.

## Diagnostic Questions

- What does a normal week of supervision look like?
- How often do trainees meet the advisor, and who drives the agenda?
- What happened to the last several trainees at the user's level?
- How are authorship, project ownership, and conference opportunities decided?
- What happens when a project fails, a trainee struggles, or the advisor disagrees with the trainee's career goal?
- Who can the user ask outside the advisor's direct influence?

Risk statement:

> The science is a strong fit, but the evidence on mentoring is incomplete. Before accepting, verify funding duration, authorship norms, current-trainee morale, and whether former trainees would choose the lab again.

## Examples

- Strong signal: three former trainees independently describe timely feedback, clear authorship, and support for different career paths.
- Warning signal: current students avoid direct answers, describe "thick skin" as necessary, or say people succeed only if they already know how to manage the PI.
- Missing evidence: the advisor says students are independent but cannot name how they help a struggling first-year student build structure.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook for general lab-management advice from a PI's perspective. Use it only when the user is choosing, evaluating, or navigating a mentoring relationship for their own career.
