# Interviews

Use this when the user is preparing for scientific job interviews, industry interviews, postdoc interviews, faculty campus visits, informational interviews, or first-round screens.

## The job of this topic

Turn preparation into role-specific evidence stories and questions. Good interview help connects the user's experience to the reader's criteria and uses the interview to test fit in both directions.

## Default workflow

1. Identify interview type, role, audience, format, stage, and known criteria.
2. Research the organization, lab, department, products, funding model, recent work, team structure, and likely interviewers.
3. Prepare evidence stories for technical skill, independence, collaboration, failure, conflict, leadership, communication, and motivation.
4. Map each story to the role's criteria using problem, action, result, learning, and relevance.
5. Prepare questions about expectations, resources, team structure, supervision, evaluation, success metrics, and next steps.
6. End with a practice plan, answer bank, question list, or risk areas to rehearse.

## Decision rules

- If the user lacks a requested skill, answer with candor, adjacent evidence, and a plan to close the gap.
- If the interviewer asks about conflict or failure, show ownership, learning, and changed behavior.
- If expectations are unclear, ask concrete questions about first-year priorities and success measures.
- If preparing for faculty interviews, align seminar, chalk talk, one-on-one meetings, teaching, and department fit.
- If the user is interviewing the lab or employer as well, include questions that reveal culture, support, and constraints.

## Variants and Edge Cases

- For industry interviews, prepare stories about teamwork, decision-making, timelines, customers, and cross-functional communication.
- For postdoc interviews, ask about project ownership, funding, authorship, independence, and job-market support.
- For faculty visits, prepare different versions of the message for specialists, non-specialists, students, administrators, and potential collaborators.
- For informational interviews, focus less on impressing and more on learning daily work, success metrics, and paths into the role.

## Anti-Patterns

- Memorizing generic answers without role evidence.
- Talking only about what the user wants rather than what the role needs.
- Treating questions for the interviewer as an afterthought.
- Hiding gaps instead of handling them directly.
- Giving the same scientific talk to every audience.

## Diagnostic Questions

- What criteria will this interviewer likely score?
- Which stories prove the user's fit for those criteria?
- What does the user need to learn before accepting or continuing?
- Which answers need practice because they involve weakness, conflict, or transition?
- What should be different for a technical interviewer, hiring manager, trainee, or department chair?

## Output Patterns / Mini-Templates

Evidence story:

| Prompt | Problem | Action | Result | Learning | Role relevance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Collaboration | Two teams had incompatible data standards | Built shared schema and review cadence | Reduced rework | Align assumptions early | Cross-functional research role |

Question pattern:

> What would success look like for this role in the first six months, and what usually prevents new hires from reaching that point?

## Examples

- Strong answer preparation pairs each likely question with one concrete story and one sentence of relevance.
- Strong interview question asks for examples of expectations in practice, not only stated values.
- For a faculty chalk talk, the story should move from problem to program to first experiments to resources needed.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook as a substitute for document review when the interview problem is caused by weak CV, resume, or application materials.
