# Faculty Search

Use this when the user is applying for faculty or group-leader roles, preparing application documents, planning interviews, or negotiating a faculty offer.

## The job of this topic

Help the user present a coherent future research program and evaluate fit with the institution. Faculty-search advice should connect documents, talks, meetings, and negotiation to the same question: can this candidate succeed here?

## Default workflow

1. Identify institution type, department needs, field, search stage, and required documents.
2. Define the candidate's independent research identity, next five-year program, technical edge, funding path, and fit with local resources.
3. Align the cover letter, research statement, teaching or mentoring evidence, CV, diversity/service material if requested, and references.
4. Prepare interview messages for different audiences: search committee, department, students, administrators, collaborators, and non-specialists.
5. For offers, rank startup, personnel, space, equipment, salary, moving support, start date, teaching load, protected time, and tenure-clock terms.
6. When the user is deciding whether faculty work fits, compare autonomy, idea ownership, flexibility, team leadership, funding pressure, management load, and alternative careers.
7. End with document-level edits, interview preparation, missing evidence, career-fit framing, or a negotiation plan.

## Decision rules

- If the application package reads like past accomplishments only, revise it to show a future program and why the institution can support it.
- If the candidate's independence from mentors is unclear, add evidence of intellectual ownership, future directions, and distinct contributions.
- If the department's needs are visible, tailor fit without pretending to be someone else.
- If an offer is on the table, negotiate for resources needed to succeed, not only salary.
- If the user has multiple offers, compare success conditions and constraints, not only prestige.
- If the user is unsure about faculty life, test the actual work: leading a research program, mentoring people, raising funds, handling uncertainty, and owning the long-term agenda.

## Variants and Edge Cases

- For research-intensive searches, prioritize research vision, funding trajectory, independence, and technical fit.
- For teaching-intensive searches, make teaching evidence concrete and connect it to the institution's students and curriculum.
- For interdisciplinary searches, explain which department or unit is the real home for evaluation and support.
- For startup negotiation, ask for enough detail to connect equipment, personnel, and protected time to the proposed program.
- For faculty-career fit, include positive reasons to pursue the path as well as risks; autonomy and team-building can be real fit signals.

## Anti-Patterns

- Writing one generic package for all institutions.
- Treating the cover letter as a summary of the CV rather than a fit document.
- Ignoring teaching, mentoring, or service expectations when the institution values them.
- Negotiating a list of wants without explaining how each item reduces execution risk.
- Accepting vague assurances about space, personnel, or protected time.
- Presenting faculty work as either heroic freedom or inevitable misery instead of a concrete bundle of responsibilities and tradeoffs.

## Diagnostic Questions

- What is the candidate's independent research program in one sentence?
- What will be possible in this department that would not be possible everywhere?
- Which evidence shows the candidate can lead, fund, mentor, and finish work?
- Which parts of the package contradict or dilute the central case?
- What resources are required for success in year one, year three, and before tenure review?
- Which parts of running a lab does the user actually want: ideas, people, grants, teaching, institutional service, autonomy, or long-horizon ownership?

## Output Patterns / Mini-Templates

Faculty package diagnosis:

| Component | Current job | Main weakness | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| Research statement | Shows past work | Future program unclear | Add specific aims, first hires, first grants, and local fit |

Negotiation ask:

> I am asking for X because it enables Y part of the proposed research program and reduces Z execution risk in the first two years.

Faculty-fit comparison:

| Work dimension | Attractive evidence | Risk evidence | Alternative path to compare |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lab leadership | Wants to build people and agenda | Dislikes management ambiguity | Senior scientist or staff scientist |

## Examples

- Strong faculty fit: the package shows a future program, uses local resources honestly, and proves independence without dismissing prior mentors.
- Weak faculty fit: the materials list publications but never explain what the lab will do after hiring.
- Strong negotiation: bundled asks with priorities and rationale, leaving room for tradeoffs.
- Strong career-fit reasoning: the user can name which faculty responsibilities they want, which they tolerate, and which risks require mitigation.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook for postdoc applications unless the user is explicitly preparing for a faculty-track transition.
