# Postdoc Applications

Use this when the user is deciding whether to do a postdoc, finding labs, applying, comparing offers, or evaluating whether a postdoc will launch the next career stage.

## The job of this topic

Make the postdoc an intentional career move. The answer should define what the postdoc must accomplish, then evaluate labs by mentorship, project ownership, independence, resources, outputs, and job-market support.

## Default workflow

1. Ask what the postdoc is for: new field, new method, publication body, independence, funding, faculty-search launch, industry bridge, or geographic constraint.
2. Build a target-lab list from scientific fit, mentor record, trainee outcomes, resources, and the user's constraints.
3. Prepare outreach that states fit, evidence of readiness, proposed direction, and why the lab is specifically relevant.
4. Evaluate offers by project ownership, funding, authorship, mentoring cadence, alumni outcomes, career support, and whether work can move with the trainee.
5. Compare risks explicitly: prestige versus support, autonomy versus structure, high-impact project versus survivability, and career launch versus personal constraints.
6. Organize active searches as a pipeline: target labs, contact reason, fit evidence, questions, offer terms, risks, and next action.
7. End with an outreach plan, question list, comparison table, search pipeline, or accept/decline recommendation with missing evidence.

## Decision rules

- If the user cannot say what the postdoc is supposed to accomplish, define that before ranking labs.
- If a lab is famous but former trainees report weak support, treat that as a major career-launch risk.
- If project ownership, funding, or authorship is vague, clarify before accepting.
- If the user wants a faculty job, check independence, portable niche, mentor advocacy, and evidence of job-market support.
- If the user is likely to leave academia, compare whether a postdoc adds relevant evidence or delays the transition.

## Variants and Edge Cases

- For cold outreach, keep the message short, specific, and evidence-based.
- For geographic or family constraints, widen the search mode before lowering the standard for mentoring quality.
- For industry-curious users, compare postdoc benefits against roles that offer direct industry experience.
- For cross-field moves, value mentors with a record of training people into the new area.

## Anti-Patterns

- Doing a postdoc because it is the default next step.
- Choosing by lab name while ignoring trainee outcomes.
- Accepting a project that cannot support the user's next application.
- Assuming verbal assurances about authorship, funding, or portability are enough.
- Treating suffering as proof that the postdoc is serious training.

## Diagnostic Questions

- What must be true at the end of this postdoc for it to have worked?
- What will the user own intellectually?
- What resources, data, organisms, equipment, personnel, or collaborations are required?
- How many recent trainees reached the kind of next role the user wants?
- How does the PI support fellowship applications, faculty searches, or non-academic moves?

## Output Patterns / Mini-Templates

Postdoc goal statement:

> This postdoc should give me independent ownership of a tractable project, training in X method, two strong outputs, and a mentor who will support my next move into Y.

Offer comparison row:

| Lab | Postdoc job | Ownership | Support evidence | Main risk | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lab A | Build new method niche | Medium | Alumni placed well | Project portability unclear | Ask directly before accepting |

Search pipeline row:

| Lab | Career goal served | Contact reason | Fit evidence | Questions | Risk flags | Next action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lab B | Build independent method niche | Recent work uses target approach | Two papers plus relevant dataset experience | Ownership, funding, portability | Authorship unclear | Ask PI and alumni |

## Examples

- Strong fit: the PI can name what the trainee will own, how feedback happens, and how former postdocs moved to similar roles.
- Weak fit: the project is exciting but depends on a senior trainee's unfinished work and no one can explain authorship.
- Useful outreach: one paragraph on shared scientific fit, one on evidence of readiness, one specific question about openings or project direction.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook for PhD rotations or faculty hiring except where mentor evaluation overlaps. Route those cases to the appropriate playbook.
