# Authorship And Contributions

Use this when the user asks for author contribution statements, acknowledgements, contribution wording, or careful language around authorship-sensitive manuscript text.

## The job of this topic

This topic helps word contribution and acknowledgement text clearly while respecting roles, consent, policy boundaries, and relationship sensitivity.

## Default workflow

1. Separate the artifact type: author contributions, acknowledgements, funding, conflict statement, or dispute-adjacent wording.
2. Identify supplied contributions and avoid inferring unprovided roles.
3. Use contribution language that names concrete work rather than status, seniority, or vague help; prefer controlled role labels when the venue requires them.
4. Keep acknowledgements distinct from authorship credit.
5. Check whether permission is needed for named acknowledgements, because acknowledgement can imply endorsement.
6. Flag policy-sensitive questions and advise checking journal, institution, or group agreements.
7. Protect privacy for people, participants, or groups who should not be identifiable.
8. For dispute-adjacent wording, keep the prose factual, role-based, and non-accusatory.

## Decision rules

- If the user asks who should be an author, do not adjudicate; help map contributions to the relevant policy criteria and suggest a transparent discussion.
- If someone is thanked for feedback, facilities, funding, or administrative support, use acknowledgement language rather than authorship language.
- If contribution wording is vague, revise toward concrete verbs such as conceptualized, analyzed, supervised, funded, drafted, reviewed, curated, or performed.
- If AI-assisted tools were used for writing, drafting, data collection, analysis, or figure generation, draft disclosure language in the appropriate section and keep humans responsible for final content.
- If the user wants to list an AI tool as an author, do not do that; provide acknowledgement or methods disclosure wording instead.
- If a dispute is active, keep the language neutral and recommend documenting contributions and consulting appropriate policy channels.
- If identifiable private people are mentioned, ask whether permission has been obtained or use a generic role.
- If a contribution statement mixes roles and praise, remove praise and keep auditable actions.
- If the venue requires controlled roles, map user-provided facts to controlled role names without adding roles.

## Variants and edge cases

- Some journals require a controlled contribution taxonomy; keep wording compatible with that format.
- Thesis acknowledgements can be more personal than manuscript acknowledgements, but still need privacy and professionalism.
- Thesis acknowledgements can be organized as major, big, and minor thanks before drafting.
- Group or facility acknowledgements may be better than naming every individual.
- Funding and institutional support should follow venue requirements rather than improvised prose.
- Large collaborative projects may need group-level contribution language plus individual role detail where required.

## Anti-patterns

- Using acknowledgements to compensate someone who likely meets authorship criteria.
- Listing authorship based on hierarchy without contribution detail.
- Thanking confidential participants in identifiable ways.
- Turning a contribution statement into praise rather than documentation.
- Giving legal or institutional determinations beyond the supplied policy context.

## Diagnostic questions

- Is this authorship, contribution documentation, or acknowledgement?
- Which roles are known from the user-provided facts?
- Does the wording imply responsibility for work the person did not do?
- Does any named person need permission or anonymization?
- Which policy or venue requirement should govern the final wording?
- Is the wording documentation, gratitude, disclosure, or dispute-sensitive communication?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

- Contribution statement: `[Name/initials] contributed to [specific role], [specific role], and manuscript review.`
- Acknowledgement: `We thank [role/person/group] for [specific support], and [facility/funder] for [specific resource].`
- AI disclosure: `The authors used [tool/type] for [specific assistance]. All authors reviewed, edited, and take responsibility for the final content.`
- Dispute-safe note: `Based on the described contributions, document each role and compare them against the journal and institutional authorship criteria before finalizing order or inclusion.`
- Role-mapping note: `Known facts -> possible contribution role -> wording caveat -> policy item to verify.`

## Examples

Before: `We thank A for helping a lot with the project.`

After: `We thank A for advice on the experimental design and comments on an earlier manuscript draft.`

## When not to apply this

Do not decide authorship eligibility, author order, or legal responsibility. Keep the output to wording, documentation, and policy-aware questions.
