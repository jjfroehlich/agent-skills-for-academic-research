# Thesis Writing

Use this when the user is planning or revising a thesis or dissertation introduction, chapter structure, discussion, conclusion, acknowledgements, or front/back matter.

## The job of this topic

Thesis writing must satisfy institutional structure while telling a coherent research story across chapters. It usually needs more context than a paper but still benefits from clear claims, chapter jobs, and bounded conclusions.

## Default workflow

1. Ask for institutional or program requirements if structure, formatting, or required chapters are uncertain.
2. Identify the thesis type: monograph, paper-based, methods-heavy, review-heavy, or mixed.
3. Map the expected arc: front matter, introduction, literature context, methods, results, discussion, conclusion, references, and appendices.
4. Define the job of each chapter and how it advances the thesis question.
5. Keep introduction, discussion, significance, and conclusion jobs distinct: introduce the problem, interpret findings, explain value, then synthesize the answer.
6. Keep conclusions focused on synthesis, contribution, limits, and future work.
7. Treat acknowledgements as public-facing professional prose with optional personal warmth.
8. Add cross-chapter bridges when papers or projects do not obviously cohere.

## Decision rules

- If institutional requirements conflict with generic guidance, follow the institution.
- If the introduction becomes a full literature review, separate background synthesis from the problem and aims.
- If a conclusion introduces new evidence, move it to results or discussion.
- If chapters read as isolated papers, add cross-chapter transitions and a unifying research question.
- If acknowledgements identify private people or participants, use permission-aware or generic wording.
- If a significance section lists broad benefits, specify the field, society, institution, or future researchers who benefit and the mechanism by which they benefit.
- If a conclusion says future work is needed for the thesis to stand, reframe future work as extension, confirmation, or boundary testing.
- If the discussion repeats results, rewrite it as interpretation, relation to literature, limitations, and implications.
- If the conclusion repeats the discussion, compress it to thesis-level answer, contribution, limits, future work, and final takeaway.

## Variants and edge cases

- Paper-based theses need framing chapters that explain how the papers cohere.
- Monograph theses need stronger chapter-level signposting because readers cannot rely on journal article structure.
- Methods-heavy theses should distinguish method development, validation, and biological or scientific use cases.
- Qualitative or human-subjects theses may need extra privacy care in acknowledgements and examples.
- Review-heavy theses should name the selection logic and conceptual contribution, not only summarize topics.

## Anti-patterns

- Treating the thesis as a long paper without institution-specific requirements.
- Ending with a broad impact claim that the chapters do not support.
- Repeating the entire discussion in the conclusion.
- Adding new data or arguments in the final chapter.
- Writing acknowledgements that create privacy or relationship problems.

## Diagnostic questions

- What requirements does the program impose?
- What is the thesis-level question or contribution?
- What does each chapter contribute to that question?
- Which chapter provides context, which provides evidence, and which provides synthesis?
- Where are the boundaries of the evidence?
- What should the examiner remember at the end?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

- Thesis introduction arc: `Field context -> unresolved problem -> significance -> thesis question -> approach/chapter map -> contribution.`
- Conclusion arc: `Answer to main question -> synthesis across chapters -> contribution -> limitations -> future work -> final takeaway.`
- Paper-based bridge: `Chapter [N] addresses [subquestion], which connects to the thesis question by [link].`
- Significance arc: `Research problem -> literature contribution -> practical or social value -> specific beneficiaries -> future research value.`
- Chapter job table: `Chapter -> job -> evidence used -> contribution to thesis question -> transition to next chapter.`

## Examples

Before: `This thesis studied several aspects of cell regulation.`

After: `This thesis asks how regulatory state changes before stable cell identity decisions, combining population-level profiling, single-cell validation, and perturbation analysis across three linked chapters.`

## When not to apply this

Do not override university, department, supervisor, or committee requirements.
