# Discussions

Use this when the user is revising a discussion, conclusion, limitations paragraph, implications paragraph, or overclaiming language.

## The job of this topic

A discussion interprets the findings. It should answer the research question, relate the answer to existing knowledge, explain implications, acknowledge limits, and close with a bounded takeaway.

## Default workflow

1. State the central answer or interpretation early, often in one paragraph rather than a full results recap.
2. Connect the interpretation to the most relevant evidence already presented.
3. Compare with prior work at the level needed for synthesis, including whether results support, challenge, extend, or complicate earlier findings.
4. Explain unexpected results and plausible alternatives before choosing the most evidence-bound interpretation.
5. Name limitations as boundaries on interpretation, not apologies.
6. Convert broad implications into evidence-bounded claims.
7. Identify what the results do not imply, especially for mechanism, population, clinical use, policy, or generality.
8. For result-by-result discussion, give each major finding an interpretation, exception or alternative, relation to prior work, implication, and clincher.
9. End with a concise contribution, implication, or next-step direction that follows from the data.

## Decision rules

- If the discussion opens by repeating results in order, replace the opening with the main answer.
- If a claim outruns the evidence, narrow the population, system, mechanism, timescale, or certainty.
- If limitations are scattered after every result, consolidate them around interpretation boundaries.
- If future work undermines the contribution, reframe it as a specific next question enabled by the study.
- If the conclusion introduces new data, move it to Results or remove it.
- If recommendations sound mandatory, soften them to evidence-bound should/could language unless the evidence and venue support stronger wording.
- If a discussion does not say where results have no implications, add the boundary condition that matters most.
- If the paragraph uses "prove", "demonstrate", "transform", or "first", check whether the design and evidence justify that strength.
- If limitations read like a weakness list, rewrite them as interpretation boundaries tied to design, measurement, sampling, or context.
- If future work sounds necessary before the current study matters, reframe it as extension, confirmation, mechanism testing, or boundary testing.
- If a paragraph jumps from finding to broad impact, insert the missing reasoning step or reduce the impact claim.
- If multiple interpretations are plausible, name the alternatives and state which one the supplied evidence supports best.

## Variants and edge cases

- Short papers may combine discussion and conclusion; keep both interpretation and takeaway visible.
- Thesis discussions can include broader synthesis across chapters.
- Null or mixed results need a careful account of what can and cannot be concluded.
- Methods or resource papers should discuss utility, validation boundaries, and appropriate use cases.
- Thesis conclusions should be shorter and more general than discussion chapters: answer the question, synthesize contribution, mention key limits, and look forward without new data.
- Mechanistic discussions should distinguish observed association, inferred mechanism, and directly tested mechanism.

## Anti-patterns

- Restating the results section without interpretation.
- Claiming clinical, policy, or mechanistic importance from limited evidence.
- Treating limitations as a perfunctory list detached from interpretation.
- Ending with a generic call for "more research" instead of a specific next step.
- Introducing new evidence in the conclusion.

## Diagnostic questions

- What question does the discussion answer?
- What is the strongest supported interpretation?
- Where does the evidence stop?
- Which prior-work comparison actually changes the interpretation?
- What plausible alternative explanation must be acknowledged?
- What does this study explicitly not show?
- What should the reader remember after the final paragraph?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

- Discussion opener: `Together, these results show that [bounded interpretation], suggesting [specific implication] in [context].`
- Limitation sentence: `Because [design/sample/measurement boundary], these data support [claim] but do not establish [stronger claim].`
- Future-work sentence: `A next step is to test whether [specific mechanism or boundary] holds in [new context].`
- Alternative-explanation sentence: `Although [finding] could reflect [alternative], [specific evidence] makes [bounded interpretation] more plausible in [context].`
- Prior-work bridge: `This finding extends [prior result] by showing [new boundary/context], but differs from [other work] in [specific condition].`
- No-implication boundary: `These data do not address [stronger/general claim], because [design or evidence boundary].`
- Result-by-result paragraph: `[Finding]. This pattern suggests [bounded interpretation]. One exception/alternative is [boundary]. In relation to prior work, [synthesis]. Therefore, [local implication/clincher].`

## Examples

Before: `These findings are very important and will transform the field.`

After: `These findings identify a reproducible association between X and Y in this model, providing a basis for testing whether the same relationship explains variation in primary samples.`

Before: `Future studies are needed to confirm these findings, and more work is required.`

After: `A next step is to test whether the same response occurs in primary samples, which would define the boundary of the association observed in this model.`

## When not to apply this

Do not turn results paragraphs into full discussions when the user needs a clean Results section.
