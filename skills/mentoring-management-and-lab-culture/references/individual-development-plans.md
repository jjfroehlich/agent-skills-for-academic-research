# Individual Development Plans Playbook

## Use this when...

Use this playbook for IDPs, yearly planning meetings, annual mentoring reviews, goal setting, career-development conversations, progress reflection, and two-way mentor feedback.

## The job of this topic

Turn development into a predictable mentoring routine. The meeting should celebrate progress, surface goals and constraints, translate ambitions into actions, and create a safe-enough venue for feedback both ways.

## Default workflow

1. Prepare: ask the trainee to reflect on progress, goals, skills, constraints, and desired support.
2. Celebrate: start with accomplishments, strengths, and learning.
3. Diagnose: identify growth areas, project risks, unclear expectations, relationship tensions, and career uncertainties.
4. Plan: translate personal and professional goals into concrete milestones, support, introductions, practice, and deadlines.
5. Repair: ask what the mentor or lab should change.
6. Document: record actions, owners, timelines, and the next review point.

## Decision rules

- If goals changed, adjust projects and mentoring rather than forcing the old plan.
- If feedback is sensitive, name power asymmetry and offer channels for later or third-party follow-up.
- If the plan is too broad, reduce it to the next quarter's actions plus a longer-horizon direction.
- If career goals do not directly serve the PI's project, still identify development steps that support the trainee's next move.
- If the trainee is discouraged, separate project outcomes from personal worth and identify what was learned.

## Variants and edge cases

- First-year trainee: emphasize onboarding, role clarity, core skills, and support routes.
- Mid-program trainee: emphasize project ownership, publication or output plan, collaboration, and decision standards.
- Late-stage trainee or postdoc: emphasize launch plan, independence, network, funding, leadership, and career options.
- Staff member: emphasize role growth, authority, recognition, skills, and workload boundaries.

## Anti-patterns

- Treating the IDP as paperwork.
- Measuring only productivity.
- Avoiding career goals outside the PI's preferred path.
- Asking for feedback without a follow-up mechanism.
- Making a plan that lacks owners, dates, or support.

## Diagnostic questions

- What does the trainee want to become?
- What skills, relationships, outputs, and evidence does that path require?
- What has changed since the last plan?
- What expectation or support has been unclear?
- What should the mentor do more of, less of, or differently?
- What will be reviewed at the next check-in?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

Yearly planning agenda:

- Progress and wins.
- Strengths to build on.
- Growth areas.
- Project and skill goals.
- Career direction and options.
- Support, introductions, and resources.
- Mentor feedback and lab-dynamic repair.
- Timeline, owners, and next review.

Two-way feedback prompts:

- "What support helped you most this year?"
- "Where were my expectations unclear?"
- "What lab dynamic made your work harder?"
- "What do you want more or less of from me?"
- "What will I change before our next check-in?"

## Examples

- A student wants a nonacademic path: plan transferable skills, introductions, project outputs, and timeline without treating the path as disloyal.
- A postdoc needs independence: shift from task delivery to decision ownership, mentoring practice, and external visibility tied to contribution.
- A trainee has repeated stress around feedback: add feedback preferences, processing time, and follow-up routes to the plan.

## When not to apply this

Do not use an IDP to manage misconduct, harassment, safety violations, or employment actions. Use the conflict and performance workflows for those issues.
