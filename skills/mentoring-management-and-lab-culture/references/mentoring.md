# Mentoring Playbook

## Use this when...

Use this playbook when the user needs mentor-mentee expectations, mentor behavior norms, co-mentoring routes, trainee support, hidden-curriculum teaching, or mentoring-malpractice diagnosis.

## The job of this topic

Make mentoring precise enough for the person in front of the mentor and plastic enough to change over time. The mentor's job is not only to supervise work; it is to make expectations, standards, opportunities, and support routes explicit in an unequal relationship.

## Default workflow

1. Identify the trainee role, career stage, project stage, and dependency on the mentor.
2. Ask what the trainee wants to become and what support, feedback, contacts, practice, and independence they need next.
3. Write down expectations for meetings, communication, feedback, authorship, project ownership, career goals, support routes, and review timing.
4. Add more than one advice route when the mentor lacks expertise, has a conflict of interest, or the trainee needs independent guidance.
5. Revisit the plan at predictable intervals and after changes in goals, performance, funding, health, project direction, or relationship quality.

## Decision rules

- If expectations are implicit, write the expectations before escalating criticism.
- If the issue involves mentor power, dependency, funding, authorship, or career access, name the power asymmetry directly.
- If the mentor cannot provide the needed advice or is part of the conflict, add co-mentors, committee members, program leaders, or institutional support.
- If a trainee is stuck, distinguish missing skill, missing information, project mismatch, unclear expectation, overload, isolation, and avoidant feedback.
- If feedback is hard, pair it with concrete next steps, support, and a chance for the trainee to respond.

## Variants and edge cases

- Undergraduates need role scope, grading or credit expectations, supervision cadence, safety basics, and short-cycle feedback.
- Graduate students need project ownership, milestones, authorship norms, training plan, committee or co-mentor routes, and career exploration.
- Postdocs need independence expectations, leadership opportunities, funding and authorship strategy, mentoring responsibilities, and launch planning.
- Staff need role clarity, authority boundaries, professional development, lab-management responsibilities, and reporting routes.
- Collaborators need decision rights, handoffs, authorship, data/code ownership, communication cadence, and conflict routes.

## Anti-patterns

- One-size-fits-all mentoring.
- Assuming the trainee wants the PI's career path.
- Blocking outside advice.
- Using kindness to avoid necessary feedback.
- Using high standards without visible support.
- Treating productivity as development when the trainee is not gaining skill, judgment, autonomy, or career options.

## Diagnostic questions

- What has been written down, and what has only been implied?
- What does the trainee think success looks like?
- What decision standards, tacit rules, or career realities has the mentor not explained?
- What advice should come from someone other than the PI?
- Is this a mentoring problem, a project-design problem, a resource problem, or a lab-culture problem?
- What would the trainee be safer saying to a third party than directly to the mentor?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

Mentoring compact:

- Meetings: frequency, agenda owner, cancellation norms.
- Communication: channels, response windows, emergencies, after-hours boundaries.
- Project ownership: responsibilities, decision rights, documentation.
- Feedback: format, timing, how hard feedback is delivered, how the trainee can respond.
- Authorship and credit: criteria, order discussions, revision timing.
- Development: skills, goals, introductions, leadership opportunities.
- Support routes: co-mentors, committee, program staff, institutional resources.
- Review cadence: when the compact is revisited.

Hidden-curriculum prompt:

"Here is the standard I used in that decision. The strong part was ____. The risk was ____. Next time, practice ____ and bring me ____ so we can compare options."

## Examples

- A trainee is missing deadlines, but no deadline norm exists: start by resetting the system, then discuss the behavior.
- A postdoc needs industry options: add external informational interviews, project leadership practice, and a launch timeline instead of measuring only papers.
- A student avoids the PI after harsh criticism: create a repair conversation and an alternate support route before returning to technical feedback.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook as a substitute for institutional procedures involving harassment, discrimination, retaliation, safety violations, visa issues, employment law, or clinical care. Use it to structure support and route the issue.
