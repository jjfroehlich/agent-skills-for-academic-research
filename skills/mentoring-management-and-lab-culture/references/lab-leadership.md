# Lab Leadership Playbook

## Use this when...

Use this playbook when the user is starting, scaling, or repairing a research group; hiring or onboarding lab members; planning capacity; running a state-of-lab review; evaluating people; or designing collaboration systems.

## The job of this topic

Translate PI responsibility into systems. Good lab leadership is not a personality trait; it is a set of routines, criteria, documents, meetings, and feedback loops that let people do strong science without guessing how the lab works.

## Default workflow

1. Name the leadership problem: hiring, onboarding, capacity, project portfolio, meeting structure, evaluation, collaboration, conflict, or morale.
2. Define behavioral lab values and connect them to decisions.
3. Check capacity: funding, PI time, supervision load, equipment, administrative support, project complexity, and collaborator commitments.
4. Create routines: one-on-ones, group meetings, project reviews, state-of-lab updates, decision logs, and follow-up.
5. Make evaluation criteria explicit before evaluating people, papers, projects, or candidates.

## Decision rules

- If ambition exceeds capacity, sequence or trim work rather than transferring unmanaged scarcity to trainees.
- If hiring, define role, rubric, questions, independence level, mentoring load, and onboarding before candidate comparison.
- If onboarding, treat the first month and first quarter as culture design, not paperwork.
- If evaluating contribution, assess rigor, role, usefulness, reproducibility, collaboration, and concrete output rather than prestige or visibility.
- If collaborating, align goals, decision rights, protected effort, authorship, data/code ownership, and handoffs at the start.

## Variants and edge cases

- New PI: prioritize role definitions, meeting rhythms, purchasing and staffing constraints, mentoring expectations, and project triage.
- Growing lab: watch for loss of informal knowledge, unclear authority, uneven access to the PI, and hidden overload.
- Interdisciplinary or computational project: involve needed specialists early and reserve time for analysis, software, data stewardship, and coordination.
- State-of-lab review: surface funding, staffing, publication pressure, institutional constraints, career concerns, and group priorities without offloading anxiety.

## Anti-patterns

- "Excellence" criteria invented after seeing candidates.
- Prestige proxies standing in for contribution.
- Adding people before supervision capacity exists.
- Treating leadership training or outside advice as a weakness.
- Assuming culture emerges automatically from good intentions.

## Diagnostic questions

- What decision is the lab repeatedly making ad hoc?
- What work depends only on the PI's memory?
- What are people afraid to ask because the answer is implicit?
- What constraint is being treated as an individual's motivation problem?
- Which group members have access to opportunity, credit, and visibility?
- What will be true in three months if no system changes?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

State-of-lab agenda:

- Funding and resource picture.
- Staffing and supervision capacity.
- Project portfolio: continue, pause, stop, or hand off.
- Publication and collaboration pressure points.
- Career-development needs.
- Policy or handbook updates.
- Decisions, owners, and review dates.

Hiring rubric:

- Role purpose.
- Required skills.
- Trainable skills.
- Independence expected.
- Collaboration needs.
- Mentoring and supervision load.
- Evaluation criteria and evidence.
- Onboarding plan.

## Examples

- A lab wants to add two students while the PI is overcommitted: assess funding, meeting time, project readiness, senior-member support, and onboarding load before recruiting.
- A collaboration is tense because computational work was added late: reset goals, roles, credit, data/code ownership, and handoff points.
- A candidate looks impressive by institution and journal names: return to predefined criteria and concrete contributions.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook for scientific project selection alone. Use the research-strategy lens when the main question is what science to pursue rather than how to lead the group doing it.
