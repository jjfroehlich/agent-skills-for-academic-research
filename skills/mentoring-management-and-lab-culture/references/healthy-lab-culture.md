# Healthy Lab Culture Playbook

## Use this when...

Use this playbook for lab health, belonging, meeting climate, opportunity and credit patterns, sustainable work, whole-person support, diversity and participation, psychological safety, and status-driven behavior.

## The job of this topic

Make culture observable. A healthy lab is built through expectations, meeting design, workload norms, credit habits, repair routes, and leadership ownership, not through friendliness alone.

## Default workflow

1. Identify the culture problem as a behavior pattern, incentive, missing routine, or unclear expectation.
2. Ask who owns the system and who experiences the burden.
3. Audit participation, opportunity, credit, visibility, workload, meeting norms, and help routes.
4. Turn the desired value into a recurring practice.
5. Add feedback and review loops so the practice can be corrected.

## Decision rules

- If belonging is the goal, design participation in decisions, meetings, opportunities, and feedback; do not stop at welcoming language.
- If diversity is relevant, ask whose perspectives are missing and what blind spots that creates.
- If work-life balance is the issue, document boundaries, flexibility, holidays, vacation, workload review, and after-hours norms.
- If a person is distressed, stay non-clinical: name observations, express care, ask what support is wanted, adjust work where appropriate, and route to qualified resources.
- If status is driving behavior, separate contribution, credit, visibility, and prestige.

## Variants and edge cases

- Productive friction can improve reasoning, but it needs meeting norms that keep disagreement claim-focused and respectful.
- Whole-person support requires boundaries: the lab can adjust workload and connect resources, but it is not a clinical service.
- Credit practices should include visible gratitude, contribution tracking, and explicit recognition for shared work.
- Flexible work still needs predictable obligations for meetings, safety, collaboration, deadlines, and handoffs.

## Anti-patterns

- Treating lab health as an optional extra.
- Judging commitment by physical presence or constant availability.
- Calling discomfort "rigor" when feedback is vague, personal, or humiliating.
- Letting high-status people capture credit.
- Treating representation as optics rather than part of scientific reasoning and opportunity access.

## Diagnostic questions

- Who speaks, decides, gets credit, and receives opportunity?
- What behavior is rewarded even if the stated values oppose it?
- What recurring conflict points to a missing policy?
- What work-life boundary exists in writing and in actual practice?
- What support route exists for distress, conflict, or retaliation concerns?
- Where is the lab confusing visibility with value?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

Lab-health review:

- Meeting climate.
- Workload and boundaries.
- Opportunity and credit.
- Onboarding and expectations.
- Feedback and repair.
- Support and escalation.
- Actions, owners, and review date.

Distress-support conversation:

"I have noticed ____. I am concerned about you, not only the work. What support would be useful right now? We can discuss workload or timeline changes, and I also want to connect you with qualified confidential resources. Here is what I can and cannot keep private in my role."

## Examples

- A lab says it values balance but rewards late-night responsiveness: rewrite communication norms and model response boundaries.
- A meeting has silent junior members: redesign turn-taking, pre-read options, dissent prompts, and follow-up channels.
- A collaboration gets public visibility from one person: track contributions and agree on credit before the next external communication.

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook to provide therapy, legal advice, HR determinations, or emergency support. Use it to structure non-clinical support and route to qualified help.
