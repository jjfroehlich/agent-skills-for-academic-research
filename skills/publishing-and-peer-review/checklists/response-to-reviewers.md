# Response-To-Reviewers Checklist

Use before sending a revised manuscript and response letter.

## Completeness

- Every reviewer and editor point is present.
- Sub-points are preserved rather than merged into vague responses.
- Each response is classified as accepted change, clarification, new analysis, new experiment, claim narrowing, disagreement, or out-of-scope request.
- Repeated comments are handled consistently.

## Traceability

- Each accepted change names the manuscript location.
- New analyses and experiments are distinguished from wording changes.
- The response states what changed, not only that a change was made.
- The opening summary names the major revision package.

## Disagreement And Scope Control

- Disagreements acknowledge the concern before explaining the rationale.
- Constraints are stated plainly without blaming the reviewer.
- If requested work was not done, the manuscript claim or limitation was revised where needed.
- Reviewer misunderstandings triggered manuscript clarification.
- Conflicting reviewer requests are explained to the editor.

## Tone

- The response is calm, specific, and professional.
- The response does not attack reviewer competence.
- The response avoids overpromising unperformed work.
- The response helps reviewers and editors verify the revision quickly.

## Final Pass

- Can the editor see the main changes in the first page?
- Can each reviewer find the answer to their own comment?
- Are unresolved constraints explicit?
- Does the response letter and revised manuscript tell the same story?
