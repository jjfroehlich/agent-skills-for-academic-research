# Peer Reviewing

Use this when the user is deciding whether to accept a review invitation, reading a manuscript as a reviewer, drafting a peer-review report, or revising reviewer comments for constructiveness and scope.

## The job of this topic

Make peer review useful to the editor and authors. A good review is fair, prioritized, evidence-linked, actionable, timely, and bounded to the manuscript's claims.

## Default workflow

1. Decide whether to accept: expertise, conflict, deadline, access to material, and ability to write a respectful review.
2. First read for the manuscript's question, central claim, contribution, and journal fit.
3. Second read for evidence: methods, data quality, figures/tables, statistics, limitations, and whether conclusions follow.
4. Sort issues into major concerns that affect validity or interpretation and minor concerns that affect clarity, presentation, or polish.
5. Draft a fair summary before criticism so the editor and authors can see what you understood.
6. Write each major comment as concern, evidence, consequence, and bounded requested action.
7. For statistical concerns, check direct comparisons, experimental units, independence, uncertainty, circularity, exploratory framing, and multiplicity before asking for more analysis.
8. Put confidential recommendations, conflicts, or ethics concerns in the editor-only channel when journal policy supports that split.
9. Final-check tone, feasibility, and whether the review would still be acceptable if signed or published.

## Decision rules

- If you lack expertise, have a conflict, or cannot meet the deadline, decline promptly and suggest alternatives when useful.
- If the paper is weak but salvageable, focus on the few validity or interpretation changes that would most improve it.
- If the concern is only style or preference, keep it minor and avoid making it a condition of publication.
- If an experiment would become a new project, ask whether the claim can instead be narrowed, caveated, clarified, or tested with existing data.
- If statistics are outside your competence, flag the concern and suggest statistical review rather than inventing objections.
- If the journal asks for confidential comments, use them for editor-only synthesis or sensitive issues, not for comments authors need to answer.
- If reviewing a revision, evaluate whether required changes were addressed and avoid adding unrelated new demands unless a new decisive issue appears.

## Variants and edge cases

- **Very poor manuscript:** Still write a professional review. State the main validity or fit barriers without sarcasm or pile-on details.
- **Likely accept:** Do not stop at praise. Identify residual limitations, clarity fixes, or checks the editor should know.
- **Revision review:** Compare the revised manuscript against the prior decision letter and major unresolved issues; do not restart review from scratch unless the journal asks.
- **Portable or transferred review:** Reconsider comments in the context of the new journal's criteria and any changed manuscript.
- **Statistical paper or heavy analysis:** Check direct comparisons, independence, assumptions, uncertainty, multiplicity, and whether claims match the analysis.

## Anti-patterns

- Accepting reviews despite conflicts, missing expertise, or impossible timelines.
- Writing a review as a list of reactions with no priority.
- Demanding citations, analyses, or experiments that mainly serve reviewer preference.
- Using anonymity to be dismissive, sarcastic, or personal.
- Telling authors the accept/reject recommendation when the journal expects that to remain confidential.
- Copyediting before assessing claims, methods, and evidence.

## Diagnostic questions

- Can I review this competently and on time?
- Can I summarize the manuscript's main claim in one or two sentences?
- Which issues would change the editor's decision?
- Are my major requests necessary for the current claim, or am I expanding the project?
- Does each criticism include evidence and a feasible action?
- Are statistical comparisons direct and are units of analysis independent?
- Would I stand behind this wording if the review were signed or published?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

**Review report skeleton**

```text
Summary
[Neutral summary of question, approach, and central claim.]

Major comments
1. Concern: [issue]
 Evidence: [where it appears]
 Consequence: [why it affects validity, interpretation, or fit]
 Requested action: [bounded revision, analysis, claim limit, or clarification]

Minor comments
- [clarity, figure, terminology, or presentation issue]

Confidential comments to editor, if needed
[Decision-relevant synthesis, conflict disclosure, ethics concern, or recommendation according to journal policy.]
```

**Constructive major comment pattern**

```text
The manuscript claims [claim], but [evidence/method/detail] does not yet support that scope. A bounded way to address this would be to [specific action], or to narrow the claim to [supported version].
```

**Statistical concern pattern**

```text
The comparison appears to test each condition separately rather than directly comparing the conditions. Please clarify the analysis strategy or provide a direct comparison aligned with the stated claim.
```

## Examples

- Instead of "The authors need many more animal experiments," write "The current data support the cellular mechanism but not the organism-level claim. Please either add evidence that directly supports that claim or narrow the conclusion to the tested system."
- Instead of "This is badly written," write "The introduction does not identify the gap until paragraph four, which makes the contribution hard to evaluate. Move the gap and central claim earlier."

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook for author response letters except to understand what a reviewer comment is asking for. Use the response-to-reviewers playbook for author-facing replies.
