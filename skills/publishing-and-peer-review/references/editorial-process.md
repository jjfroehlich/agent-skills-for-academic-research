# Editorial Process

Use this when the user needs to interpret an editor decision, communicate with an editor, understand review routing, or decide what an editorial request really means.

## The job of this topic

Explain editorial decisions as process judgments that combine scope, audience, novelty, evidence, reviewer input, journal constraints, and timing. Keep advice realistic without treating editors as either adversaries or advocates.

## Default workflow

1. Identify the decision point: presubmission, desk decision, review invitation, revision request, appeal, transfer, or final decision.
2. Separate editor signals from reviewer signals.
3. Categorize the editor's concern: scope, article type, audience, novelty, practical value, validation strength, reporting readiness, ethics, logistics, or reviewer synthesis.
4. Decide whether the user should clarify, revise, appeal, transfer, or move on.
5. If contacting the editor, draft one focused question and include only enough context for the editor to answer.
6. Preserve a professional tone and avoid implying entitlement to review or acceptance.

## Decision rules

- If the editor asks for clarification, answer the exact process or scope question before adding new persuasion.
- If revision expectations are unclear, ask a specific question rather than guessing or submitting a mismatched package.
- If reviewer requests conflict, tell the editor how you plan to balance them and ask only when the conflict affects the revision strategy.
- If an editor says the paper is outside scope, do not treat more rhetoric as the default fix.
- If a conversation with an editor occurs before submission, use it to understand fit and standards, not to seek special treatment.
- If reviewer requests would expand the project, separate conclusion-changing work from optional extensions and explain the filtered revision plan.

## Variants and edge cases

- **Desk rejection:** The editor may be signaling fit, novelty, audience, or presentation problems before peer review. Diagnose each possibility.
- **Major revision:** The decision is not acceptance. Treat it as a bounded negotiation with required changes and residual risk.
- **Conflicting reviewers:** Editors often need the authors to explain a defensible path through the conflict.
- **Appeal:** Appeals are narrow. They should be based on a specific decision-relevant error, not general disagreement.
- **Editor relationship:** Prior conversations can improve clarity but do not replace manuscript quality or journal criteria.

## Anti-patterns

- Assuming reviewer preference and editor decision are identical.
- Sending long emails with multiple buried questions.
- Treating editors as obstacles instead of decision-makers balancing journal scope and community value.
- Using editor contact to pressure for a favorable decision.
- Ignoring journal-specific instructions because an editor previously seemed interested.

## Diagnostic questions

- What exactly did the editor decide or request?
- Which part of the decision came from the editor rather than a reviewer?
- Is the concern about fit, evidence, novelty, ethics, or process?
- Are the manuscript's reporting, data, software, and claims ready for the decision being requested?
- What answer would let the editor make the next decision faster?
- Is there a legitimate process error, or only a disappointing outcome?
- Would a short clarification email reduce risk before revision?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

**Editor clarification note**

```text
Dear [Editor],

Thank you for the decision and guidance. Before we finalize the revision, we would like to clarify one point: [specific question]. Our proposed approach is [brief plan]. Would this address the journal's concern, or would you prefer [alternative]?
```

**Decision interpretation**

```text
Editor signal: [scope/evidence/novelty/process]
Reviewer signal: [main reviewer issue]
Practical meaning: [what must change]
Recommended next move: [revise/clarify/appeal/transfer/move on]
Residual risk: [remaining uncertainty]
```

## Examples

- "The reviewers ask for two additional analyses, but the editor highlights claims that are too broad. The safest revision is to narrow the claim and add one feasible analysis, then explain why the second request is outside the current scope."
- "This looks like a scope rejection, not a quality rejection. The next move is to revise the cover letter and target a venue whose readers need the method."

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook to infer hidden editor motives or to replace journal-specific instructions. When policy matters, follow the journal's explicit process.
