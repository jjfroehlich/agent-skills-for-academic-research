# Journal Submission

Use this when the user is preparing a submission package, cover letter, presubmission inquiry, appeal, transfer, or resubmission to a new journal.

## The job of this topic

Turn publication anxiety into a concrete process decision. The agent should check fit, completeness, and decision history before drafting text or recommending an appeal.

## Default workflow

1. Identify the manuscript stage: first submission, presubmission inquiry, revision, transfer, appeal, or new submission after rejection.
2. Check journal fit: scope, article type, audience, novelty threshold, method/data expectations, and practical requirements.
3. Check package completeness: manuscript files, figures, supplemental material, declarations, author information, data/code statements, reviewer suggestions, and any journal-specific forms.
4. Draft the cover letter around the central claim, evidence strength, audience fit, article type, and required declarations.
5. If suggesting reviewers, require a short expertise rationale and screen for conflicts; do not present suggestions as a way to steer the decision.
6. If there was a prior rejection or review history, decide whether to disclose it, revise first, transfer, appeal, or submit elsewhere.
7. End with the next action and the largest remaining risk.

## Decision rules

- If journal scope or article type is a poor match, choose a better-fitting venue instead of relying on cover-letter persuasion.
- If the user's question is whether the work fits, use a focused presubmission inquiry when the journal welcomes it.
- If the paper was rejected, classify the reason before acting: scope, novelty, evidence, presentation, process error, factual misunderstanding, or reviewer overreach.
- If the rejection contains valid reviewer concerns, revise the manuscript before sending it elsewhere unless timing makes that impossible.
- If appealing, argue only a clear factual, process, or interpretation error and keep the appeal short.
- If submitting a previously reviewed version elsewhere, do not hide changes that matter to the new evaluation; use the cover letter or response package when disclosure is appropriate.
- If a prior rejection is relevant, do not rely on cosmetic title or abstract changes; make material revisions before treating the manuscript as a new submission.

## Variants and edge cases

- **Fast submission deadline:** Run only the readiness checklist, fix missing declarations and fatal scope mismatches, and avoid cosmetic overwork.
- **Trainee included in editor communication:** Prepare the specific question and boundaries before the call or email so the exchange is educational rather than performative.
- **Conflicting advice from coauthors:** Separate journal-fit evidence from preference. Decide based on scope, audience, and what the current manuscript can defensibly claim.
- **Rejection after review:** Treat reviewer comments as reusable diagnostic input even if the next journal is different.
- **Desk rejection:** Diagnose fit, novelty, title/abstract presentation, and missing contextual framing before assuming the science was rejected.

## Anti-patterns

- Sending a generic cover letter that could be addressed to any journal.
- Asking an editor whether a broad project is interesting without a specific scope or article-type question.
- Treating personal contact with an editor as a substitute for meeting journal criteria.
- Appealing every rejection.
- Resubmitting immediately elsewhere while ignoring valid concerns that will recur.
- Overstating novelty, priority, or certainty beyond what the manuscript supports.

## Diagnostic questions

- What is the central claim, and is it visible in the title, abstract, and cover letter?
- Why is this journal's audience the right audience?
- Which journal requirement is most likely to block technical acceptance?
- Are declarations, data/code statements, author details, and supplemental files complete?
- Is the appeal or resubmission decision based on evidence, or only frustration?
- What would an editor need to know in the first paragraph to route the paper correctly?

## Output patterns / mini-templates

**Cover-letter skeleton**

```text
Dear Editor,

We submit [article type], "[title]," for consideration. The manuscript reports [central claim] based on [core evidence or method]. We believe it fits [journal/audience] because [specific fit].

The work contributes [novelty or practical value]. [Required declarations, prior review/transfer note if appropriate, data/code availability if relevant.]

Thank you for considering the manuscript.
```

**Presubmission inquiry skeleton**

```text
We are preparing a [article type] on [central claim]. The work uses [brief evidence] and is aimed at readers interested in [audience]. Could you advise whether this appears within the journal's scope before we prepare a full submission?
```

**Rejection diagnosis table**

| Decision signal | Likely meaning | Next move |
|---|---|---|
| Out of scope or audience | Journal mismatch | Choose a better venue or send a focused inquiry elsewhere |
| Insufficient novelty | Positioning or venue mismatch | Revise framing or lower novelty threshold |
| Major evidence gap | Claim not yet supported | Revise, narrow claims, or add feasible evidence |
| Factual/process error | Decision may rest on mistake | Consider concise appeal |

## Examples

- "The decision letter says the work is technically sound but not broad enough. I would not appeal first; I would identify a venue where the audience is narrower, then revise the title, abstract, and cover letter to match that audience."
- "The editor's concern is not just formatting. The article type requires a resource description, but the current package reads like a research article. Fix article-type fit before submission."

## When not to apply this

Do not use this playbook for line editing, figure design, literature review, or grant strategy unless the user also needs a concrete journal-process decision.
